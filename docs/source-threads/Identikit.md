## Identity-Kit: Conceptual Framework

**Core tension you're exploring**: identity as both _fixed system_ (biometric, algorithmic, archival) and _unstable construction_ (performed, contested, undone).

* * *

### **1. Conceptual Grounding**

An identity-kit inherits from:

*   **Bertillon/police composite systems** → identity as measurable, decomposable, reconstructible
*   **Algorithmic face recognition** → identity as data surface, reproducible, gamifiable
*   **Identity politics scholarship** → identity as performative, contextual, multiple
*   **Speculative design** → "what if we made the system visible/interactive?"

Your interrogation: _What happens when you make identity-construction itself interactive?_ Who controls the kit? What gets standardized? What escapes?

* * *

### **2. Modular Architecture**

**Theoretical layer** (publishable):

*   Situate within critical biometrics / surveillance studies literature
*   Map the genealogy: analog composites → digital systems → your intervention
*   Name what you're _not_ doing (e.g., not enabling surveillance, not essentializing identity)
*   State the speculative premise clearly

**Interactive layer** (the experience):

*   User builds/decomposes an identity through choices (facial features, gestures, markers, metadata, etc.)
*   System shows: what emerges, what's lost, what the system standardizes vs. what resists
*   Possible structures: collage, slider-based morphing, questionnaire-to-portrait, generative variation, archive-browsing, removal/addition of traits

**Technical layer** (rendering):

*   Could be: web-based (p5.js, Three.js, ML5 face detection), desktop app (Processing, Python + Pygame), browser with Canvas/WebGL
*   Could pull from: generative face libraries, historical photo archives, user-uploaded imagery, algorithmic generation
*   Modularity: separate identity _construction_ (data layer) from _rendering_ (visual layer) from _interaction_ (control layer)

* * *

### **3. Research Methodology**

Structure it around:

1.  **Genealogy**: How have identity-kits been used historically? What assumptions did they encode?
2.  **Critical analysis**: What do your choices in the design reveal about identity systems?
3.  **User/audience study**: How do people interact? What do they discover or critique through use?
4.  **Reflexivity**: What does _making_ the kit teach you about identity construction itself?

This becomes your scholarship—publishable as paper + artifact.

* * *

### **4. Deliverables (Publication-Ready)**

*   **Academic paper**: genealogy + critical analysis + methodology (target: digital humanities, STS, or art/theory journal)
*   **Interactive artifact**: documented code, playable/installable, reproducible
*   **Exhibition/presentation**: contextual statement, technical specs, interaction documentation
*   **Dataset/archive** (optional): what identities emerged, how were they constructed, metadata about choices

* * *

### **5. Quick Technical Decision Tree**

*   **Low-code / prototyping-first**: p5.js (Face detection, visual variety, web-deployable)
*   **High-fidelity rendering**: Three.js + morphing/geometry shaders
*   **Archival/curatorial**: web interface querying a database of images + metadata
*   **Generative**: ML models (StyleGAN, diffusion) to produce variation
*   **Hybrid**: Combination (e.g., user choices → generate → render → reflect)

Each has different reproducibility, scalability, and exhibition implications.
